# Motor Control Fundamentals

If sensors are a robot's eyes and ears, motors are its muscles. Every physical action a robot takes—moving across a room, picking up an object, turning its head—requires motors to convert electrical signals into physical motion. Understanding how motors work and how to control them is fundamental to building robots that can act effectively in the world.

This chapter explores the different types of motors used in robotics, the principles of motor control, and the feedback systems that enable precise, reliable movement.

## The Challenge of Motion

Moving in the physical world seems simple—you decide to reach for a cup, and your hand moves to grab it. But this apparent simplicity masks extraordinary complexity. Your brain must:

1. **Plan the trajectory** — Where should your hand go, and how should it get there?
2. **Generate motor commands** — Which muscles should contract, how much, and when?
3. **Execute the movement** — Send signals to muscles and coordinate their activation
4. **Monitor and adjust** — Use sensory feedback to correct errors along the way

Robots face all these same challenges. The motor control problem is fundamentally about turning desired movements into actual movements, despite all the uncertainties and disturbances of the physical world.

## Types of Electric Motors

Electric motors are the most common actuators in robotics because they're efficient, controllable, and available in a wide range of sizes and capabilities. Each type has distinct characteristics that make it suitable for different applications.

### DC Motors: Simple and Versatile

The **Direct Current (DC) motor** is the simplest type of electric motor. Apply voltage across its terminals, and it spins. Reverse the voltage, and it spins the other way. The speed is roughly proportional to voltage, and the torque is roughly proportional to current.

**How DC Motors Work:**
A DC motor has two main components: the **stator** (stationary part) which creates a magnetic field, and the **rotor** (spinning part) which carries electrical current. When current flows through the rotor windings in a magnetic field, it experiences a force that causes rotation.

The ingenious part is the **commutator** and **brushes** that automatically switch the current direction in the rotor as it spins, keeping the motor rotating smoothly in one direction.

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Simple control**: Speed proportional to voltage
- **Good torque at low speeds**: Useful for many robotic applications
- **Bidirectional**: Reverses by reversing voltage polarity
- **Moderate efficiency**: 75-80% typical
- **Maintenance required**: Brushes wear out over time

DC motors are found in everything from toy cars to industrial robots. They're often the first choice when simplicity and cost matter, and they work well for moderate-precision applications.

### Brushless DC Motors (BLDC): Efficient and Durable

**Brushless DC motors** eliminate the mechanical commutator and brushes, instead using electronic switching to alternate current in the motor windings. This seemingly simple change brings significant advantages.

**How BLDC Motors Work:**
In a BLDC motor, the permanent magnets are on the rotor, and the current-carrying windings are on the stator. Electronic circuits sense the rotor position and switch current between windings at precisely the right times to keep the motor spinning. This is called **electronic commutation**.

**Advantages over brushed DC:**
- **Higher efficiency**: 85-95% due to reduced friction and electrical losses
- **Longer lifespan**: No brush wear means less maintenance
- **Better power-to-weight ratio**: Can be lighter for the same power output
- **Lower noise**: No brush arcing or mechanical contact

**Trade-offs:**
- **More complex control**: Requires electronic speed controller (ESC)
- **Higher cost**: Both motor and controller are more expensive
- **Requires position sensing**: Needs to know rotor position for commutation

BLDC motors dominate in applications where efficiency, reliability, and power density matter—drones, electric vehicles, computer disk drives, and high-performance robotics.

### Servo Motors: Precision Positioning

A **servo motor** is not a distinct motor type but rather a motor packaged with position feedback and a controller. When you command a position, the servo automatically moves to that position and holds it.

**Components of a Servo:**
1. **Motor**: Usually a small DC or BLDC motor
2. **Gearbox**: Reduces speed and increases torque
3. **Position sensor**: Typically a potentiometer or encoder
4. **Control electronics**: Compares commanded vs actual position and drives the motor

**Hobby Servos:**
The small servo motors common in hobbyist robotics accept a **PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) signal** where the pulse width encodes the desired position:
- 1000 microseconds → 0 degrees (one extreme)
- 1500 microseconds → 90 degrees (center)
- 2000 microseconds → 180 degrees (other extreme)

These servos are inexpensive and easy to use but have limited rotation range (typically 180 degrees) and modest precision.

**Industrial Servos:**
Higher-end servo systems use high-resolution encoders, powerful motors, and sophisticated controllers that can achieve positioning accuracy measured in micrometers. They're essential for manufacturing robots, CNC machines, and any application requiring precise, repeatable motion.

### Stepper Motors: Open-Loop Precision

**Stepper motors** move in discrete angular steps—typically 200 steps per revolution, or 1.8 degrees per step. By counting steps, you can control position without any feedback sensor (open-loop control).

**How Stepper Motors Work:**
A stepper motor has multiple electromagnetic poles arranged around the rotor. By energizing these poles in sequence, the rotor "steps" from one position to the next, attracted to whichever poles are currently energized.

**Step Modes:**
- **Full step**: Energize one or two poles, 1.8° per step
- **Half step**: Alternate between one and two poles, 0.9° per step
- **Microstepping**: Vary current smoothly between poles, achieving 1/16, 1/32, or even 1/256 of a step

**Advantages:**
- **No feedback required**: Position known by counting steps (if no steps are missed)
- **Excellent holding torque**: Maintains position when stationary
- **High precision**: Microstepping can achieve very fine positioning
- **Repeatability**: Returns to same position reliably

**Limitations:**
- **Can miss steps**: Under high load or acceleration, may skip steps and lose position
- **Resonance**: Certain speeds cause vibration and torque loss
- **Efficiency drops at high speed**: Torque falls off as speed increases
- **Always draws current**: Uses power even when stationary

Stepper motors are ideal for 3D printers, CNC machines, and applications where precise positioning matters but speeds are moderate and loads are predictable.

## The Fundamentals of Motor Control

Having a motor is only the beginning. The real challenge is making it do exactly what you want—move to a specific position, maintain a constant speed, apply a controlled force. This is the domain of **control theory**.

### Open-Loop vs Closed-Loop Control

**Open-loop control** means sending a command without checking the result. You calculate what input *should* produce the desired output and apply it, trusting that your model is accurate.

*Example:* To spin a motor at 100 RPM, you calculate the required voltage based on the motor's specifications and apply it. If the motor has a load, if it's heating up, if the battery voltage drops—you won't know, and the actual speed will drift from your target.

**Closed-loop control** means measuring the output and adjusting the input to correct errors. You compare what you wanted with what you got, and modify your command to close the gap.

*Example:* To maintain 100 RPM, you measure the actual speed with an encoder. If speed is 95 RPM (too slow), increase the voltage. If it's 105 RPM (too fast), decrease the voltage. Keep adjusting continuously.

The advantages of closed-loop control are profound:
- **Handles uncertainty**: Works even if your motor model is imprecise
- **Rejects disturbances**: Compensates for varying loads and conditions
- **Adapts to changes**: Adjusts as motors age or temperatures change

Nearly all precision robotics uses closed-loop control. The cost is complexity—you need sensors and more sophisticated control algorithms—but the improvement in performance is usually worth it.

### PID Control: The Workhorse of Robotics

**PID control** (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) is the most widely used feedback control algorithm. It's simple enough to implement on a microcontroller yet powerful enough to handle a vast range of control problems.

PID works by calculating three terms based on the **error**—the difference between what you want and what you have:

**Proportional (P):** Response proportional to current error
- Large error → large correction
- Small error → small correction
- Problem: May not eliminate steady-state error completely

**Integral (I):** Response proportional to accumulated error over time
- If error persists, integral grows, increasing correction
- Eliminates steady-state error
- Problem: Can cause overshoot and oscillation

**Derivative (D):** Response proportional to rate of change of error
- If error is changing rapidly, applies damping
- Helps prevent overshoot
- Problem: Amplifies noise in measurements

The total control output combines all three:

**Output = Kp × error + Ki × ∫error dt + Kd × d(error)/dt**

Where Kp, Ki, and Kd are **gains** that determine how strongly each term influences the output.

### Tuning PID Controllers

Getting good PID performance requires choosing appropriate gains—a process called **tuning**. Poor tuning can result in:

- **Sluggish response**: System takes too long to reach the target
- **Overshoot**: System exceeds the target before settling
- **Oscillation**: System swings back and forth around the target
- **Instability**: Oscillations grow rather than decay

**Effect of Each Gain:**

| Gain | Increasing Effect | Too High |
|------|------------------|----------|
| Kp | Faster response, smaller error | Oscillation, overshoot |
| Ki | Eliminates steady-state error | Overshoot, slow recovery |
| Kd | Reduces overshoot, dampens response | Noise sensitivity, jitter |

**Manual Tuning Approach:**
1. Set Ki and Kd to zero
2. Increase Kp until the system responds briskly but starts oscillating
3. Add Kd to dampen the oscillation
4. Add Ki to eliminate any remaining steady-state error
5. Fine-tune all three for best performance

**Ziegler-Nichols Method:**
A classic systematic approach:
1. Set Ki = 0 and Kd = 0
2. Increase Kp until system oscillates with constant amplitude (this is Ku, the ultimate gain)
3. Measure the oscillation period (Tu)
4. Calculate PID gains from formulas:
   - Kp = 0.6 × Ku
   - Ki = 1.2 × Ku / Tu
   - Kd = 0.075 × Ku × Tu

These formulas provide a starting point that usually works reasonably well, though fine-tuning is often needed.

## Motor Driver Electronics

Motors typically need more current and voltage than a microcontroller can provide directly. **Motor drivers** act as power amplifiers, taking low-power control signals and switching high-power motor currents.

### H-Bridge for DC Motors

The **H-bridge** is the standard circuit for controlling DC motors. It uses four switches (typically transistors) arranged in an H pattern:

```
     +V
      │
  ┌───┴───┐
  │       │
 [Q1]   [Q3]
  │       │
  ├── M ──┤
  │       │
 [Q2]   [Q4]
  │       │
  └───┬───┘
      │
     GND
```

By controlling which switches are closed:
- **Q1 and Q4 on**: Current flows left-to-right through motor → forward rotation
- **Q2 and Q3 on**: Current flows right-to-left through motor → reverse rotation
- **Q1 and Q3 on** (or Q2 and Q4): Short circuit! This damages the circuit—never do this.
- **All off**: Motor coasts (freewheels)
- **Q2 and Q4 on**: Motor terminals shorted, providing braking

Speed control is achieved by **PWM**—rapidly switching the transistors on and off. A 50% duty cycle means the motor sees 50% of the supply voltage on average.

### Stepper Motor Drivers

Stepper motors require sequencing current through multiple windings. Stepper drivers handle this sequencing, accepting simple step and direction signals:

- **STEP signal**: Each pulse advances the motor one step
- **DIR signal**: High or low determines rotation direction
- **ENABLE signal**: Enables or disables the driver

Modern stepper drivers also implement **microstepping** by varying the current in each winding smoothly, providing finer positioning and quieter operation.

## Cascaded Control: Position, Velocity, and Torque

For precise robot control, multiple control loops are often nested in a **cascaded** structure:

**Inner Loop - Current/Torque Control (fastest, ~10+ kHz):**
Controls the motor current, which determines torque. This loop compensates for motor electrical dynamics.

**Middle Loop - Velocity Control (~1 kHz):**
Takes velocity commands and outputs torque commands to the inner loop. Compensates for load variations and friction.

**Outer Loop - Position Control (~100 Hz):**
Takes position commands and outputs velocity commands to the middle loop. Handles trajectory tracking and settling.

The principle is that each outer loop sets the target for the inner loop, and each inner loop runs faster than its outer loop. This separation makes each controller simpler and the overall system more stable.

**Why Cascade?**
A single position controller trying to directly command motor voltage would need to handle all the dynamics at once—electrical, mechanical, and motion. By separating concerns:
- The inner current loop handles fast electrical dynamics
- The velocity loop handles mechanical dynamics
- The position loop handles motion planning

Each loop can be tuned independently, and problems are easier to diagnose and fix.

## Practical Considerations

### Gear Reduction

Motors spin fast but produce little torque. Most robotic applications need the opposite—slower movement with higher torque. **Gear reduction** trades speed for torque:

- **10:1 gear ratio**: Output spins at 1/10 the speed, with 10× the torque (minus losses)
- **100:1 ratio**: 1/100 speed, ~100× torque

Gear reduction also reduces effective motor inertia as seen from the load, making the system easier to control.

Common gear types in robotics:
- **Spur gears**: Simple, efficient, but can have backlash
- **Planetary gears**: Compact, high ratios, good efficiency
- **Harmonic drives**: Zero backlash, high ratio, expensive
- **Worm gears**: Very high ratio, self-locking, lower efficiency

### Backlash and Compliance

**Backlash** is the slack or play in a gear train—the amount you can rotate the output shaft before the input shaft starts moving. Backlash causes positioning error and can make control systems unstable.

**Compliance** is flexibility in the mechanical system. While backlash is generally undesirable, some compliance can be beneficial—it can absorb impacts and enable safer human-robot interaction.

### Thermal Considerations

Motors generate heat from electrical resistance and mechanical friction. If they overheat:
- Permanent magnet motors can demagnetize
- Windings can burn out
- Lubricants can break down

Motor drivers also generate significant heat, especially when handling high currents. Proper heatsinking and thermal management are essential for reliable operation.

## Key Takeaways

1. **DC motors** are simple and versatile; **brushless motors** offer better efficiency and longevity; **servos** provide integrated position control; **steppers** enable open-loop precision.

2. **Closed-loop control** is essential for precision—it handles uncertainty, rejects disturbances, and adapts to changing conditions.

3. **PID control** combines proportional, integral, and derivative action to achieve fast response with minimal overshoot and zero steady-state error.

4. **Proper tuning** is crucial—poorly tuned systems can be sluggish, oscillatory, or unstable.

5. **Motor drivers** amplify control signals to power levels motors need.

6. **Cascaded control** separates fast inner loops (current/torque) from slower outer loops (velocity/position) for easier tuning and better performance.

7. **Mechanical design** matters—gear ratios, backlash, compliance, and thermal management all affect system performance.
